The Argument · 1,400 words

This isn't a vibes problem.
It's a data problem.

Loneliness in young men isn't a meme. It's a measurable health crisis, and the apps that promised to fix it are statistically making it worse. Don't Die Alone exists because you deserve actual feedback — not another swipe down at 1 a.m., not another monologue from a friend who is too polite to tell you which photos are killing your match rate.

The receipts: Twenty-nine percent. That's the increased risk of premature death from social isolation, equivalent in mortality terms to smoking up to fifteen cigarettes a day, per the U.S. Surgeon General's 2023 advisory. Twenty-five percent of young American men report feeling lonely "a lot of the day," the largest gap between young men and the rest of the developed world, according to the Gallup World Poll.

The number of men reporting no close friends at all has gone up five-fold since 1990 — from 3% to 15%, per the Survey Center on American Life. Fifty-two percent of men on Hinge get fewer than one match per day. Thirty-eight percent of dating-app users developed symptoms consistent with clinical depression over a twelve-week study.

The dating-coach industry, meanwhile, runs on $99 monthly subscriptions, vibes, and screenshots of mid messages. The pickup artist scene is worse. None of them will tell you what the data actually says.

The problem is not you. The problem is that nobody has ever shown you the math on what's actually working in your photos and what isn't.

Section II · The method

How it works.

i.

Upload

Drop in your photos and any bio screenshots. We figure out which is which automatically.

ii.

Pick your apps

Tinder, Hinge, Bumble — twelve supported. Or pick up to four at once for a multi-app stack recommendation.

iii.

We score it

Every photo gets graded on Photofeeler-style dimensions: smile, eye contact, lighting, framing, background, authenticity. Plus 24 detected boolean flags. Your bio gets scanned for red-flag phrases and conversation hooks. Your prompts get bucketed: humor, depth, conversation-starter, cliché.

iv.

Read the fix list

Top fixes ranked by estimated match-rate lift, with the published research backing each one. Plus an optional rewrite of your bio in your chosen voice — Coach or Roast.

Section III · The instrument

What the engine does.

§ 01

Per-platform scoring across twelve apps.

Your Tinder lead photo is scored differently than your Hinge lead — because the data says they should be. Each platform has its own weighted rubric.

§ 02

Photofeeler-style photo grading.

Six dimensions per photo, scored 0–10. Plus 24 detected boolean flags: sunglasses, mirror selfie, fish, exotic car, group photo, full-body, et cetera.

§ 03

Multi-platform best-photo-picker.

Upload up to twenty photos. Get the recommended six for Bumble, nine for Tinder, six for Hinge — in one pass, with swap-in alternates.

§ 04

Bio and prompts breakdown.

Per-prompt verdict, specificity flag, one-line critique. Plus a polished rewrite that fixes every flagged issue.

§ 05

Coach or Roast voice.

Same diagnosis, different delivery. Coach is constructive. Roast is witty with teeth — never cruel, never punching down.

§ 06 · The flag

A separate Fuck-Boy Detector.

A discrete 0–100 score that flags red-flag patterns women told researchers they swipe left on. Gym mirror. Exotic car. Suit-and-cigar. "No drama" bios.

§ 07

A multiplier, not a vibe.

Every score converts to an estimated match-rate multiplier. "Bumble: 84/100" reads as "≈3× your baseline match rate." Concrete, not abstract.

§ 08

Save and share.

Every analysis is saved automatically. Send your result to a friend with a public link, or come back to compare a new run against a previous one.
